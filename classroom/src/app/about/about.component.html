{{ title }} @ {{ institution }} is a university-wide resource for writing education. It is an interactive text analysis and visualization environment that supports writing instruction in foundational writing courses and upper-level discipline specific courses at Carnegie Mellon University.

{{ title }} is part of Carnegie Mellon’s instructional sidecar project led by Richard Scheines, Dean of the Dietrich College of Humanities and Social Science and Marsha Lovett, Director, Eberly Center for Teaching Excellence & Educational Innovation. Sidecars for writing education embody core competencies of our foundation-level writing courses that can be delivered and adapted just-in-time to upper-level writing courses and workshops.

The core of our sidecar approach to writing is the concept of a lens—a visualization that helps students see how their writing of even a single proposal is made up of hundreds, thousands, even tens of thousands of small composing decisions below their conscious awareness. Experienced writers have enough tacit awareness of these decisions to help them with self-assessment and revision. But student writers often need cueing to become aware of this level of decision-making as a resource for self-assessment and revision. As a visualization, a lens provides these external cues.

DocuScope Classroom is built on the DocuScope Project began in 1998 as a collaboration between David Kaufer and Suguru Ishizaki in the Department of English at Carnegie Mellon University. They created a computer-aided text analysis environment called DocuScope with a suite of interactive visualization tools for corpus-based and temporal rhetorical analysis. Kaufer created what we call the generic (default) dictionary, consisting now of over 60 million unique patterns (words or word sequences) of English classified into over 43,000 categories of semantic-rhetorical effects.
